Gun violence in Chicago, a socioeconomic study

The USA is one of three countries where a constitutional right to bear arms exists and is the only country that has no constitutional restrictions. Every day, 200 Americans are shot and wounded, and in 2020 an estimated 45,222 deaths occurred from firearms. Gun violence has become an unavoidable reality of American society.

The 1996 Dickey Amendment, reduced the federal limits on government funded research on gun violence by up to 90%, motivated by the lobbyist group the National Rifle Association (NRA). Whilst changes have been made to the amendment, the NRAs political power has severely reduced research on gun violence, and as such, ways to reduce it. Data on the subject is limited, but non-profit organisations are currently trying to bridge these gaps, ultimately aiming to understand the full impact of gun violence. Scholars have investigated the root socioeconomic causes on gun violence, finding social mobility to be a driving force. Social mobility is a dynamic concept referring to how people move throughout the income or wealth distribution. This project aims to research the influence of socioeconomic causes and/or impacts in the city of Chicago, Illinois.

First, the correlation between Firearm Mortality Rates (number of deaths per 100,000 people) and per capita personal income for US States is graphed annually. R squared values are calculated finding a relationship between income and firearm deaths in the US at a state level. It must be noted that Firearm data includes deaths by all intent. Alaska, which has the 1st highest rate of gun deaths in the US, has 68% of gun deaths by suicide, compared to Illinois having a similar statistic for homicide (61%), thus correlations must be treated with caution when regarding intent.

Why Chicago?

Narrowing down the research geographically, the 10 most populated cities are identified and using data from the non-profit organisation Gun Violence Archive (GVA), which compiles data from law enforcement, media, and government sources from all 50 states. Fatal and non-fatal shootings from 2014-2022 are graphed in Figure 2. Due to the GVA's database query method, only 2000 rows of data could be collected at a time. A data analysist hand collected and cleaned comprehensive data spanning 1/1/2014 to 06/15/2022, and I hand cleaned and collected the rest, compiling the data.

Chicago, the 3rd most populated US city, has a clear gun problem, having the highest amount of non-fatal shootings yearly, despite restrictive gun laws relative to other states. Illinois is is one of seven states requiring licenses/permits to buy firearms and is one of five states requiring waiting periods for buying any firearm. Chicago borders Indiana and Wisconsin, which have relatively week gun laws, one potential reason for their failed gun laws. But can gun violence be explained by socioeconomic causes?

Mapping Gun Violence

Reported incidents of fatal and non-fatal shootings collected from the Chicago Data Portal are mapped at neighbourhood levels. Austin, which is Chicago's largest neighbourhood (in size and population), located far from the city centre suffers from the most reported gun crimes out of all the neighbourhoods, increasing by 55% from 2014-21.

Mapping income disparities

Austin has long suffered from severe disinvestment because of white flight, making it a hub of concentrated poverty, shown by its drastically different income distribution than to the City of Chicago. But can poverty be a predictor of gun violence? In the maps below, we can see that neighbourhoods with high gun crimes tend to have lower incomes, but the South-East of Chicago sees low median incomes but relatively low reported gun crimes. Thus, we also look at social mobility, seeing if children who grew up with parents in the lowest 25th percentile of national income distribution can escape poverty. The results here fit the gun crime map more consistently. Climbing up the economic ladder is harder. The once since achievable American Dream is just a fantasy for many people in Chicago, and across America. Structural inequalities, often rooted in racism, mean marginalised individuals are more likely to live in hyper segregated poor neighbourhoods with limited economic opportunity, fuelling gun violence.

other socio economic disparities

Socioeconomic disparities across Chicago are not limited to income. Educational attainment and children receiving food stamps is mapped below. Exposure to gun violence can detrimentally students' educational attainment, further reducing their economic opportunities, creating a cycle of violence for already suffering communities. High poverty concentration can have detrimental implications for the risk of youth firearm-related deaths, with tracts in Austin having over 50% of households with children under 18 receiving government Food Stamps.
