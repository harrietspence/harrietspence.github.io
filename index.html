Gun violence in Chicago, a socioeconomic study

The USA is one of three countries where a constitutional right to bear arms exists. Every day, 200 Americans are shot and wounded. Gun violence has become an unavoidable reality of American society.

The 1996 Dickey Amendment, motivated by lobbyist group the National Rifle Association (NRA), significantly reduced federal funding for research on gun violence. Despite changes to the amendment, the NRA's political power has hindered research on this important issue. This project aims to use data from non-profit organizations and scholarly research to examine the root socioeconomic causes and impacts of gun violence, with a focus on social mobility: the ability to move up the income or wealth distribution.

A negative correlation between Firearm Mortality Rates (number of deaths per 100,000 people) and per capita personal income for US states is shown below. It must be noted that data here includes deaths by all intent, which can vary significantly between states. For example, Alaska has a higher rate of gun deaths by suicide, while Illinois has a higher rate of gun deaths by homicide. Correlations should be treated with caution when considering the intent behind gun deaths.

Why Chicago?

The top 10 populated US cities are matched with data from the non-profit organisation the Gun Violence Archive (GVA), which compiles data from law enforcement, media, and government sources from all 50 states, plotting fatal and non-fatal shootings from 2014-2022.

Despite restrictive gun laws, Chicago accounts for 57% of all non-fatal shootings out of the top 10 most populated cities, and 9% of all cities and counties in the GVA dataset (2014-2022). Illinois is one of seven states requiring permits to buy firearms and is one of five states requiring waiting periods for buying any firearm. This research aims to see if socioeconomic causes can explain their ineffective gun laws.

Mapping Gun Violence

Reported incidents of fatal and non-fatal shootings collected from the Chicago Data Portal are shown at neighbourhood levels on an interactive map. Austin, which is Chicago's largest neighbourhood, located far from the city centre, suffers from the most reported gun crimes out of all the neighbourhoods, accounting for 8% of all reported crimes in the 77 neighbourhoods between 2014-22.

Mapping income disparities

Austin has long suffered from severe disinvestment because of white flight, making it a hub of concentrated poverty, shown by its drastically different income distribution than to the City of Chicago. But can poverty be a predictor of gun violence? In the maps below, we can see that neighbourhoods with high gun crimes tend to have lower incomes, but the South-East of Chicago sees low median incomes with relatively low reported gun crimes.

Social mobility is also mapped, seeing if children who grew up with parents in the lowest 25th percentile of national income distribution can escape poverty. The results here fit the gun crime map more consistently. Climbing up the economic ladder is harder. The once since achievable American Dream is just a fantasy for many people in Chicago, and across across America. Structural inequalities, often rooted in racism, mean marginalised individuals are more likely to live in hyper segregated poor neighbourhoods with limited economic opportunity, fuelling gun violence.

Other socioeconomic disparities

Socioeconomic disparities across Chicago are not limited to income. Educational attainment and children receiving food stamps is mapped below. Exposure to gun violence can detrimentally affect students' educational attainment, further reducing their economic opportunities, creating a cycle of violence for already suffering communities. High poverty concentration can have detrimental implications, increasing the risk of youth firearm deaths, and as tracts in Austin have over 50% of households with children under 18 receiving government Food Stamps, the neighbourhood is especially vulnerable to youth related gun violence.

Data Challenges

Due to the GVA's database query method, only 2000 rows of data could be collected at a time. A data analysist hand collected and cleaned comprehensive data, and I collected and hand cleaned the rest. Data cleaning overcame the issue of city names not being the same in both data sets and aggregated daily statistics into annual data.

I initially used API keys for the US census bureau data to plot poverty rates. However, the household median income dataset was easily accessible to be downloaded from their database query function. Census tracts in the North-East of Chicago have missing data for social mobility, which is a limitation. However, as this contains the richest neighbourhoods in Chicago and has high educational attainment, we assume these regions are unlikely to be outliers in economic mobility.

Conclusions

Correlations, between high levels of gun violence and low social mobility, can be seen in Chicago. However, causality may not necessarily be taken, as socioeconomic measures and gun violence may be two-way causal. Nonetheless, the research here provides beneficial insight into the impacts and causes of gun violence, a pressing issue which deserves further exploration.
